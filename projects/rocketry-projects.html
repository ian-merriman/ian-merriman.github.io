Technical Approach

One of my individual rocketry projects was a drogue-chute nose-cone design for my L1 certification rocket. I created the system after an earlier flight failed due to the parachute not deploying properly. The redesigned nose cone was built to self-deploy after the ejection charge fired, using airbrakes that splay outward to increase drag and help pull the parachute out of the body tube.

I began by modifying the original nose-cone design, shortening the shoulder section, increasing the wall thickness, and removing material in an X-pattern to create space for the airbrake arms. The airbrakes were split into four curved sections and were later reinforced with steel pivot rods instead of printed PLA pivots to reduce the chance of failure under loading.

What I Learned

These rocketry projects helped me understand that aerospace engineering is not only about analysis, but also about execution. Hardware projects require careful planning, clear documentation, safety awareness, and strong communication between team members.

The drogue-nose-cone project specifically taught me how valuable it is to design around failure modes. After the first parachute deployment issue, I redesigned the mechanism, strengthened weak points, tested the system, and verified that the final design worked in flight.

Future Improvements

In future rocketry work, I would like to expand my technical contributions in areas such as flight simulation, avionics, recovery systems, thrust vector control, and test data analysis. For the drogue-nose-cone design, future improvements could include additional ground testing, stronger material selection, refined airbrake geometry, and better quantitative tracking of deployment forces.
