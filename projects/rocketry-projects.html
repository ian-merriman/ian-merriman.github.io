Project Overview

My rocketry experience includes both team-based competition rocket work through AeroMavs and individual rocket development. For the AeroMavs competition rockets, my main contribution was on the manufacturing side, including composite body tube fabrication, fin attachment, tube cutting, sanding, and finishing.

This work helped me connect aerospace concepts to real hardware while developing practical experience with composite materials, epoxy bonding, build procedures, safety practices, recovery systems, and documentation.

My Role

My primary role in AeroMavs competition rocket work was manufacturing support. I helped fabricate fiberglass body tubes, attach fins, cut tubes to size, create coupling rings, and perform drilling, sanding, and finishing work to prepare the rocket structure for integration and competition use.

I also completed individual design work for a personal L1 certification rocket, where I developed a modified drogue-chute nose-cone system to improve parachute deployment reliability.

Technical Approach

For the AeroMavs competition rockets, I supported composite body tube manufacturing using fiberglass fabric and epoxy. The layup process began with mandrel preparation, including sanding the mandrel, applying baby powder as a release aid, wrapping the mandrel in Mylar, and adding peel ply before applying the fiberglass.

During the layup, fiberglass fabric was wrapped around the mandrel and coated with epoxy while the mandrel was rotated to keep the coating even. The process continued until approximately three to five layers of fiberglass were built up, then the body tube was left to cure for about a day.

I also supported structural finishing work by attaching fins with a mixture of epoxy and silica powder while using a jig to maintain alignment. I cut tubes to specified dimensions, created coupling rings to mate body sections, and performed drilling, sanding, and other finishing processes.

Drogue-Nose-Cone Project

This individual project focused on improving recovery reliability for my L1 certification rocket. After an earlier parachute deployment failure, I redesigned the nose cone so that it could help pull the parachute out of the body tube after ejection. The design uses four deployable airbrakes that open outward, increasing drag and helping the recovery system deploy more consistently.

I modeled the redesigned nose cone and airbrake components in SolidWorks, adjusted the shoulder geometry, added pivot slots, reinforced the mechanism with steel rods, and tested the design before flight. The final system successfully deployed during the test flight and contributed to my L1 certification through the Tripoli Rocketry Association.

What I Learned

These projects helped me understand that aerospace engineering is not only about analysis, but also about execution. Manufacturing work requires process control, patience, attention to detail, and an understanding of how small build choices can affect the final vehicle.
