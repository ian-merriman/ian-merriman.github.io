Project Overview

My rocketry project experience includes student-led aerospace work through AeroMavs and individual rocket development. Through AeroMavs, my main contribution to the competition rockets was on the manufacturing side, where I supported fiberglass body tube layups, fin attachment, tube cutting, sanding, and finishing work.

This work helped me connect classroom aerospace concepts to real hardware, team workflows, and the practical challenges that come with building and preparing rocket systems for competition. It also gave me experience with composite manufacturing, epoxy work, safety practices, recovery systems, and documentation in a hardware-focused engineering environment.

My Role

My primary contribution to the AeroMavs competition rockets was manufacturing support. I helped fabricate fiberglass body tubes using layup processes, attached fins using epoxy-based bonding methods, cut tubes to size, and performed extensive sanding and finishing work to prepare the body tubes and fin fillets.

I also completed individual design work for a personal L1 certification rocket, including a modified drogue-chute nose-cone system intended to improve parachute deployment reliability. That individual work allowed me to take ownership of a specific mechanical design problem from failure identification through redesign, testing, and flight.

Technical Approach

For AeroMavs competition rocket manufacturing, I supported the fabrication of composite body tubes using fiberglass fabric and epoxy. The layup process began by sanding the mandrel before use, applying baby powder as a release aid, wrapping a layer of Mylar around the mandrel, and adding a layer of peel ply.

After the mandrel was prepared, we added fiberglass fabric and coated it with epoxy while rotating the mandrel to apply an even coating. We continued rotating and wrapping the mandrel until approximately three to five layers of fiberglass were built up around the tube. The completed body tube was then left to cure for about a day so the epoxy could harden.

I also supported fin attachment and finishing. I attached fins to the rocket body using a blend of epoxy and silica powder while using a jig to hold the fins in position. I cut body tubes to size using a Dremel while wearing a respirator for protection, then performed extensive sanding to shape and smooth the body tube and fin-attachment fillets.

Drogue-Nose-Cone Project

This individual project focused on improving recovery reliability for my L1 certification rocket. After an earlier parachute deployment failure, I redesigned the nose cone so that it could help pull the parachute out of the body tube after ejection. The design uses four deployable airbrakes that open outward, increasing drag and helping the recovery system deploy more consistently.

I modeled the redesigned nose cone and airbrake components in SolidWorks, adjusted the shoulder geometry, added pivot slots, reinforced the mechanism with steel rods, and tested the design before flight. The final system successfully deployed during the test flight and contributed to my L1 certification through the Tripoli Rocketry Association.

What I Learned

These rocketry projects helped me understand that aerospace engineering is not only about analysis, but also about execution. Manufacturing work requires process control, patience, attention to detail, and an understanding of how small build choices can affect the final vehicle.

My AeroMavs manufacturing work taught me how composite body tubes are created through preparation, layup, curing, cutting, and finishing. It also reinforced the importance of safety when working with epoxy, fiberglass, cutting tools, and sanding operations.

My individual rocket work taught me how valuable it is to design around failure modes. After experiencing a parachute deployment issue, I redesigned the mechanism, strengthened weak points, tested the system, and verified that the final design worked in flight.
